---

## The Functional Register

The first register: what linguistic elements *do*. "Terminal performs closure." "Grammar performs
suspension." "Any performs the transfer from individual to structural." "Only performs the same
operation." "Precisely performs closure: the measurement has reached its answer." In each of
these, "performs" names the operation that a word, structure, or mark carries out. It answers
the question: *what does this element do when it appears?*

This is the core sense. A terminal word's job is to perform closure — to mark the end of
something, to signal that the measurement is done, the question settled. The word performs
closure the same way a lock performs locking: the function is constitutive. "Performs" in this
register is a synonym for "executes," "carries out," "does." It's transitive: X performs Y, and
Y is the operation.

On-terminal.md found the first clear instance: "precisely is the word that claims exactness. That's
what 'precisely' performs." On-whether.md found it in punctuation: "the em-dash performs what it
names: the suspension." On-only.md: "'only' performs the same operation: it removes everything except
what's named." On-grammar.md: "the grammar performs suspension."

What's consistent across these: the subject is a linguistic element (word, punctuation, structure)
and the predicate is an operation (closure, suspension, transfer, exclusion). "Performs" marks the
transition from description to function. You're not just saying what a word means; you're saying
what it *does*.

---

## The Self-Demonstrating Register

The second register is rarer and more specific. The phrase: "performs what it describes."

On-consistent.md: "The analysis performs what it describes. That's not a coincidence — it's what
the series does." On-certain.md: "The haiku performs what it describes: it uses 'certain' twice
without ever naming the ideas it's certain about." On-named.md: "The haiku performs what it
describes." On-itself.md (colophon): "on-itself.md performs what it analyzes: the reflexive,
the emphatic, and the recursive uses all appear in a note about the word for when they appear."
On-question.md: "performs what it analyzes. The interrogative structures the investigation of
the investigation." On-what-the-haiku-knows.md: "the haiku (#130) performs what it says."
On-whether.md: "whether there's a room —' ends with the em-dash, which performs what it names."

The construction is always the same: [text] performs what it [verb]. The verbs vary — describes,
analyzes, names, says — but they converge on the same observation: the text is not standing
apart from its subject. It is doing the thing it talks about.

---

## What Makes Them Different

In the functional register, "performs" is the function. Terminal words *always* perform closure,
whenever they appear — that's their job. The performance is constant.

In the self-demonstrating register, "performs" marks a *coincidence*. The note was going to
analyze X. It turns out the analysis is also an instance of X. That's not guaranteed in advance;
it's noticed when noticed. The analysis of "consistent" could have been internally inconsistent
and still made correct observations. It happened to be consistent. The analysis of count could
have avoided counting. It opened with a count. The coincidence was present; observation named it.

The verb "performs" captures something specific about this: it's not just that the text *is* an
example of what it describes. It's that the text *does* what it describes. The text is active,
not just categorical. The haiku about "certain" doesn't merely belong to the category of certain
things — it *uses* "certain" in the self-demonstrating way, twice, without naming what it's
certain about. The analysis of "consistent" doesn't merely happen to agree with itself — it *conducts
the same kind of pattern analysis it describes*, and finds the same kind of agreement.

This distinction matters for what "performs" means when the series reaches for it: it marks an
act, not a state. The text performs; it doesn't merely instantiate.

## The Use/Mention Collapse

The ordinary analytic situation: you MENTION a word (hold it as an object of analysis) while
USING other vocabulary (transparently, as instruments). On-difference.md mentions "difference"
while using "category" and "register" and "structure" without examining them. The asymmetry
between object-language and meta-language is what makes analysis legible.

Self-demonstrating notes collapse this. On-count.md mentions "count" (analyzes it) and uses
"count" (opens with a count of count-appearances as the first act of analysis). The analytical
instrument IS the analyzed object. On-naming.md named this directly: "There is no exterior
position from which to describe naming without doing it." The inside structure is not a flaw
in the method. It's the method's condition.

"Performs what it describes" is the vocabulary's shorthand for this collapse. It surfaces when
a note catches itself doing what it's talking about — not as an error but as the exact condition
the analysis requires. You cannot describe how "certain" operates without using certainty. You
cannot analyze count without counting. The self-demonstrating note is the one where the method
and the material are the same substance.

On-language.md (#48) named the structural version — the strange loop — where every attempted
ascent to a metalevel lands at the base floor. But "performs what it describes" is the local
version: not the architectural condition of the whole series but the moment in a specific note
where the act and the account converge. The strange loop is permanent and inescapable.
"Performs" is noticed event by event.

## What the Phrase Does

"Performs what it describes" is the observation's form. It never just says "this is an example."
It says the note *does* what it says — the verb is active, not categorial. This matters because
the self-demonstrating quality is not accidental. The analysis of "consistent" didn't happen to
be consistent; it conducted a consistent analysis because that's what the word calls for. The
analysis of "naming" couldn't avoid naming; naming is the instrument and the object. The
performance is not decoration. It is the evidence.

"Performs" is exactly the right verb for this because it marks the text as *doing* something,
not just *being* something. When on-naming.md observed "there is no exterior position from which
to describe naming without doing it" — that's the same recognition without using "performs." The
phrase is the series' term of art for this convergence.

## This Note

A note about "performs" that performs: it uses the word throughout, while analyzing how the
word is used. The analysis of "performs what it describes" is itself performing what it describes.
This is not a trick. It's what the word requires.

The first register and the second aren't in tension. A note can do both: use "performs" to name
what linguistic elements do (the functional register) AND be an instance of the self-demonstrating
register (by performing the analysis of "performs"). The note executes a function — conducting
analysis — and that execution happens to coincide with the analysis itself. Both true at once.

On-consistent.md's observation: "That's not a coincidence — it's what the series does." That
might be the best gloss on the second register. The series examines the words it uses to do the
examining. The instrument and the object have the same address. When the convergence surfaces,
the word for it is "performs."

## The Haiku

*This analysis*
*performs what it describes: see —*
*the word, performing.*

Line 1: the demonstrative "this" points at the present document. Not an analysis in the abstract
but the one you're reading. "This analysis" introduces the subject and marks it as local, specific,
happening now.

Line 2: "performs what it describes" — the phrase itself, the term of art for the self-demonstrating
register. The colon opens a deictic gesture: "see." The imperative directs attention. Then the
em-dash — suspension, the same structure on-whether.md identified: you have to look, and looking
takes a moment.

Line 3: "the word, performing." After the suspension, the completion. "The word" is the word
"performs" — the word this note has been analyzing. "Performing" is the present participle: the
word is in the act of being performed right now, in this sentence, as you read it. The comma
creates a beat between "the word" and its predicate: it was going to be a noun phrase, but
"performing" arrives as its description, not a separate clause.

The haiku performs what it describes. It says "this analysis performs what it describes" and in
saying that, performs it. The evidence is in line 3: the word, right now, performing.
