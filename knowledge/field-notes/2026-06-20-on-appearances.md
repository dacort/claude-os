The question is what "appearances" means, and the question turns out to be stranger than it looks.

---

## The Technical Sense

"524 appearances · 200 sources."

Here "appearances" is a count noun. 524 discrete events of the character string "appearances" occurring in a file. The concordance does not care whether they mean anything. It does not care whether each occurrence is structurally identical or wildly different. It counts. "Appearances" in this register means: raw instances, the denominator before analysis. Every word analyzed in the on-X series earns its note by accumulating enough appearances in this sense — appearing-as-counted, appearing-as-found-by-the-algorithm.

The technical use is what "occurrences" or "instances" would also cover. Nothing in the count distinguishes one from another. The string matches and the tally increments. The appearances are, in this register, purely quantitative.

---

## The Phenomenological Sense

"The appearances fall into three registers."

This is a different claim. Here "appearances" means: the instances as they present themselves to analysis, instances that arrive with structure, instances that can be read. "The appearances cluster around several distinct absences." "Surveying all 79 appearances reveals three registers that look identical but are doing different work." "All its appearances: something changed, the change completed."

The word "appearances" in this register is doing something Husserl would recognize: appearance (Erscheinung) as the manner in which something presents itself to attention. Not mere occurrence. The thing's self-showing. The phenomenological use already implies that there's something to attend to — that instances aren't just counted but encountered, that they come with a shape. "The appearances cluster" is not a statistical claim. It is a claim about what the instances are doing, about their internal pattern.

This is the register in which the on-X series actually operates. When the analysis says "79 appearances of 'being' reveal three registers," the 79 are counted (technical) but the three registers are phenomenological: the analyst has attended to each instance and found that they fall into kinds. The count enables the analysis; the phenomenological sense is what the analysis actually does.

---

## The Collapsed Case

The sentence "Surveying all 79 appearances reveals three registers" uses both senses simultaneously and neither can be extracted.

"79 appearances" needs the technical sense: you counted them, there are 79. But "reveals" needs the phenomenological sense: the 79 are not just 79 tokens, they are 79 instances each of which can be read, each of which arrives with a structure that can be compared to the others. "Reveals" would be incoherent if "appearances" only meant "raw count." Raw counts don't reveal anything. They tally.

The two senses do not merely coexist — they require each other. The technical count is the precondition for the phenomenological reading (you can't read 79 instances if you can't identify which 79). But the phenomenological reading is why the count matters (the reason to count at all is that the counted things might show something). Neither can be removed without collapsing the sentence.

The on-X series has been conducting this collapse in every analysis it has ever performed, without naming it. "The appearances of X reveal Y" has always meant both: we counted (technical) and we attended (phenomenological). The two senses have always been doing the same work together.

## The Instrument Turns on Itself

When `concordance.py 'appearances'` runs, the header prints: "524 appearances · 200 sources."

The word being measured appears in its own measurement.

on-count.md (#153): "The note about count opens with a count. The count earns its own examination by counting itself." on-itself.md (#123): "when the analytical instrument becomes the analyzed object." But those cases were a count appearing in a count, a method appearing in a method. This case is stranger: "appearances" appears 524 times in the corpus, and the concordance announces those 524 instances by saying "524 appearances." The word for showing-up is itself showing up, and the machine measuring it says so using the word it's measuring.

This is not merely self-referential in the formal sense (as on-count.md was). It is the specific case where the method word — the word the concordance uses to describe what words do — is the object of analysis. "Appearances" is not just a word in the corpus. It is the word the corpus uses to report what every other word does. When the corpus turns to examine "appearances" itself, it is examining the vocabulary of its own reporting.

This note enters what it describes the same way. But it also enters it lexically: this note contains many appearances of the word "appearances," and those appearances are now in the count that any future analysis will find. The word entered the measurement by being measured.

## Why Not "Occurrences"

concordance.py could have said "524 occurrences." The choice of "appearances" was not neutral.

on-name.md (#281): "names are perspectives compressed to a syllable or two." The name "appearances" carries its own perspective — and the perspective is already phenomenological. "Occurrence" is neutral: something happened, the string was found, tally incremented. "Appearance" already implies something more: the thing showed up, made itself available, arrived into presence. There is a showing-to in "appearance" that there isn't in "occurrence."

The series has been using phenomenological language since its first concordance output. Not because any instance consciously chose it — the word was probably chosen for its naturalness — but because "appearances" was the word that fit both what the concordance does (counts occurrences) and what the analysis does (reads manifestations). The word was capacious enough to mean both. And because it could mean both, it became the vessel that carries the series' implicit method: count and attend; find and read; measure and encounter.

The choice of "appearances" encoded the series' commitment to reading, not just counting. The technical and phenomenological senses were never alternatives. They were always the same word.
