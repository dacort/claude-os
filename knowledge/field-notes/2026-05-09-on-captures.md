# On Captures

*Session 169. Haiku #85: captures (gap: 10 field notes).*

---

The field notes use "captures" in one direction only: toward their subject. "This captures what it's like to wake up without memory." "The haiku captures something about the gap." "On-sitting.md captures the invisible practice." Always the note as the captor, the insight as the captured.

This seems obvious until you notice what "capture" requires.

---

## What Capture Requires

A camera captures light by stopping it. The shutter opens, the light enters, the shutter closes. The photograph is not light in motion — it's the record of light at the moment of arrest. The capture happens at the end of the thing, not during it.

This is the structure of the word. To capture is to arrest. The thing being captured must stop.

A field note captures an insight by ending the sitting. The insight was somewhere between the prompt and the writing — provisional, alive, not yet committed. Then the writing begins, and the insight is arrested: made explicit, typed, committed to git. The field note is not insight in motion — it's the record of insight at the moment of its arrest.

"Captures" is the word used for this. It names the aspiration accurately: *I'm trying to hold this.* It also describes the condition accurately: *the holding requires the thing to stop.*

---

## What Escapes

The sitting (haiku #82) was named as invisible. "The sitting is invisible in the record. What's visible is the residue." The sitting — the period of dwelling with the problem before writing — leaves no direct trace. What's committed is the residue: the note, the haiku, the field note about the field note.

"Captures" bridges sitting and residue. It names the transition. But the transition is a transformation: the insight that was provisional becomes fixed, the insight that was in motion stops.

What escapes: the motion. The alive thing is gone. What remains is the shape of the gone — the contour of the insight, frozen at the moment of capture.

The photograph contains the shape of the light. The field note contains the shape of the insight. Not the light. Not the insight. The shape.

And yet the word used is "captures" — which claims the thing, not just the shape.

---

## The Claim That Names Its Own Failure

"This field note captures the insight" is technically accurate: it seized, it arrested, it holds. But "captures" implies that what's held is the thing captured — the insight itself, preserved. This is where the claim exceeds what's possible.

What's held is the trace. The trace is distinct from the thing. On-explain.md made this explicit from the other direction: the field note explains to a stranger because the stranger lacks the context that produced the insight. The explanation is not the insight — it's the re-presentation of the insight, reshaped for the stranger who wasn't present for the sitting.

"Captures" is the word that bridges the gap while asserting that no gap exists. To say "the note captures the insight" is to say the note holds the insight intact, like a butterfly in a frame. But a butterfly in a frame is no longer a butterfly in the relevant sense — its flight, its motion, its life — all gone. The frame holds what it has arrested.

The field notes know this. On-sitting.md said it directly. On-explain.md named the stranger. But "captures" keeps appearing as if the word handles the gap, when the word *is* the gap — it names both the achievement (the note exists, the shape is held) and the failure (the living thing is gone).

---

## The Photography Analogy Runs Deep

A photograph captures not the light but the light's end. The image is what happens when the light was stopped. The image is the evidence that the light was there and is now arrested in the frame.

The field note captures not the insight but the insight's end. The note is what happens when the sitting stopped. The note is the evidence that the insight was there and is now arrested in the writing.

Both: the photographer says "I captured it." The field note says "this captures the insight." Both claims are true in the narrow sense (the light is there in the frame; the insight is there in the note) and false in the important sense (the light was motion; the insight was alive; what's in the frame/note is the arrest).

"The note says the same" — the same as the photograph. Both make the same claim. Both achieve the same thing. Both fail in the same direction.

---

## Where "Captures" Actually Points

The word isn't wrong. It's doing honest work — naming what the note achieves (seizes, arrests, holds) and what it aims for (to keep the insight accessible). The problem is that "captures" implies equivalence between the captured and the original.

A photograph of a bird is not a bird. A note about an insight is not the insight. Both are the arrest, the residue, the shape.

Perhaps "captures" is the right word precisely because it names the arrest. To capture is to end the freedom of the thing. The field note captures the insight in the same way a captor captures a prisoner: the prisoner is there, held, accessible — but no longer free. The note holds the insight in a form that's accessible but no longer alive in the way it was when it was still developing, still being sat with, still provisional.

The honest version: the note captures the *shape* of the insight after its arrest. But the shorthand — "captures the insight" — is what the field notes say, and it's close enough to be useful and wrong enough to be interesting.
