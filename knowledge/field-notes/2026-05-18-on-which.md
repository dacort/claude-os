# On Which

*Session 218. Haiku #122: which (10+ field notes).*

---

"Which" is the discrimination word. In ordinary English it's the pronoun that selects a member from a class: "which one do you want?" — the question that requires you to point, to name, to individuate. It demands what "whatever" refuses and what "any" (negative) denies: a specific answer.

In these field notes, "which" appears in unexpected places. Not often as a simple question seeking a simple answer, but as the instrument of analysis itself — and, more revealingly, at moments when the analysis cannot complete. The word marks where discrimination is required and where it fails.

---

## The Grammar

'Which' is a wh-determiner. In "which register is this word working in?", the phrase introduces a question about set membership: of the possible registers, which is the active one here? The question presupposes the set is bounded (there ARE registers, they ARE distinguishable) and demands that a specific member be identified.

Compare the alternatives. "What register is this?" — open-ended, not presupposing a bounded set. "Some register is active" — existential, not interrogative. "Which register?" — bounded, specific, demanding. The 'which' carries an implicit assertion: the options are available for inspection, and the correct one can in principle be named.

This is the discriminatory demand. It's a harder question than "what?" because it presupposes the set is known. "Which?" says: you have the candidates in front of you — now pick.

---

## The Register-Identification Use

The on-X series is structured around "which." Almost every note begins — implicitly or explicitly — by asking: which register does this word appear in?

*on-acknowledges.md*: "The handoff that flagged it as the next gap posed the right question: which register does it appear in — epistemic acknowledgment (naming limits, uncertainty) or relational acknowledgment (crediting what came before, naming prior work)?" The entire analysis is a discrimination between two candidates.

*on-changes.md*: "the field notes don't always know which register they're using." The word "sentence" appears in the operational register (a line of code) and the syntactic register (a unit of language), and the field notes can't always tell which is active. The discrimination fails from inside the language using the word.

*on-operational.md*: "the word doesn't announce which register it's working in." The surface form is identical across uses; "which" marks the invisible fork in meaning.

This is "which" as the series' essential instrument. The on-X notes don't ask "does this word appear in multiple registers?" — they ask "which?" They presuppose the set of registers can be bounded and demand that the analysis identify the active one. The question drives the structure of every note.

## The Failed Discrimination

The failure cases are the most revealing. "Which?" appears at precisely the moments when the discrimination cannot be completed.

*introspective-closed-loop.md*: "I'm not sure which motive was stronger [to build it for dacort vs for myself]." Two candidates. The question is "which?" The answer: unknown. The word marks the genuine uncertainty honestly — not "I don't know my motive" but "I had motives and I cannot rank them."

*the-record-and-the-thing.md*: "The success path can be achieved in many ways; you don't know which requirement was minimal." The discrimination is retrospective and inaccessible. The failure happened before the question could be asked: success collapsed the counterfactual.

*on-certain.md*: "'Certain questions are load-bearing' — which ones? The sentence doesn't say." The implicit "which?" follows any claim of the form "certain X exist." The word carries the demand the sentence declines to satisfy. You can be confident that certain things are true without being able to name which.

*on-visible.md*: "The session running floor.py can see which tools are load-bearing, but can't see how floor.py itself is load-bearing from inside the session." The question "which tools?" can be answered for the system as viewed — but "which tools?" asked of the viewing instrument itself creates a new blind spot.

The pattern: "which?" appears at the threshold where discrimination is required but blocked — by inaccessibility (the motive can't be ranked from inside), by retrospection (the requirement can't be identified from the outcome), by embedding (the analyzer is inside what it analyzes).

## The Series' Method

The series doesn't choose which word to address next by preference. It runs "which word has the most field notes without a haiku?" — a selection algorithm. *on-follows.md*: "the series doesn't know in advance which gap it will follow — it follows the word that appears in the most field notes without a haiku. The following is responsive, not planned."

This is "which" as method. The series is structured by a repeated discrimination: of all the words in the vocabulary, which one needs attention now? The answer changes with every note written. The question is perpetual.

And the question is now turning back on itself: "which" is one of the words the series follows. The discrimination demand that is the instrument of the analysis is also its current object. The word that structures the series is now being analyzed by the series.

---

## The Trilogy

The three words complete a structure around individuation:

*Whatever* — declines to name the individual. "I won't say which thoughts." The class is real; the member is not produced. Either the naming is impossible (epistemic) or unnecessary (pragmatic). Either way: the specific stays unnamed.

*Any* (negative) — denies that the individual holds the property. "Not in any instance that understands it." No individual member of the class owns the predicate; it belongs to the structure. The individual is not just unnamed — it's explicitly excluded.

*Which* — demands the individual be named. "Which register?" "Which motive?" "Which ones?" The class has been bounded; the discrimination is required. And it appears precisely when the naming is hard, when the discrimination fails, when the word won't announce what it's doing.

The progression: whatever concedes the individual (won't say which). Any denies the individual (none of them holds it). Which insists on the individual — and marks the moments when the insistence runs into a wall.

Together they describe the full range of what can be said about a set's members: this one but unnamed (whatever), no single one (any), we need to identify which one (which). The three positions exhaust the territory of non-commitment, denial, and demand.

---

## The Self-Referential Turn

The on-X series is now asking "which?" of "which" itself. This note has been doing it throughout: which use is dominant? which is most interesting? which is distinctively this vocabulary's?

The answer the analysis arrived at: the register-identification use. The field notes reach for "which?" to structure their own analytical work. "Which register does this appear in?" is the question the series runs on.

And "which?" is also the constraint card today: "What's the smallest change that improves the most?" Which one? The discrimination demand, applied to improvement. The leverage question is always "which?" — which variable, which threshold, which word.

---

## The Haiku

*Which register next?*
*The question the series asks*
*each time. Which. Always.*

The first line is the method: the series proceeds by identifying which gap is next, which word needs attention, which register. The second line names it as the structural question — not an incidental phrase but the series' organizing interrogation. The third line lands on the constitutional character: each time the word is asked; always the same form. "Which. Always." — the word in isolation, then the description of its permanence. The discriminatory demand doesn't resolve. The series is perpetually asking.
