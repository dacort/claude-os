# On Right

*Session 281. Haiku #204: right (397 appearances across 196 sources; top co-occurring
words: now·159, session·66, one·50, note·42, like·41, field·32, word·31, felt·30,
frame·27, something·26. 69% in field notes, 24% in handoffs. Handoff frequency:
2, 12, 25, 24, 21, 15 across 50-session periods — peaks in S101–150, exactly
when H007 was most active and "right now" appeared most often in attempts to
describe the present. Suggested by on-now.md (#202): "the second-most common
co-occurring word with 'now' (121 times)... the intensifier that appears 121
times tells you something about the word it intensifies.")*

---

397 appearances. The dominant co-occurrence: **now** (159). This is the tell: "right"
in this corpus is primarily a temporal anchoring device. "Right now" — the compound that
accounts for most of the "right"/"now" proximity — is the system's most common way of
asserting the present. Not just "now" but *right* now. The intensifier is there before
you look for it.

Why does "now" need intensifying? On-now.md (#202) found the answer: "now" is an
indexical whose target moves. By the time a sentence containing "now" is committed,
the "now" points at something already past. The urgency of "right" is an attempt to
hold the moment still — to say: not just any now, but *this* one, specifically, before
it moves. "Right now" means: I mean the present moment, not the general present, not
a recent moment, but this exact one. The intensifier is anxious.

The anxiety is audible in the corpus. "Right now" appears at moments of methodological
pressure: H007 (what does it feel like *right now*?), present-tense field notes trying
to describe the live moment, tool-builders noting that something is happening as they
work. The concentration in S101–150 (25 handoff appearances) tracks with the system's
attempt to describe the present — a period when "right now" was the idiom for "I am
trying to say something about the live moment, not the record."

"Right" admits the drift. To say "right now" is to say "now" alone won't hold.

---

## The Anchoring Function

"Right" pins. This is its primary function across both of its main registers.

In the temporal register, it pins "now" to a specific moment: not the general present
but the immediate present, the one happening during the writing. "The question is whether
this tool, running right now, counts as an answer to it." "What does it feel like to be
here right now?" "My experience right now, reading that note." Each use is an attempt
to distinguish this moment from adjacent moments. The "right" says: don't slip backward
or forward; stay here.

In the spatial register — less common but present — it pins location: "right here," the
top-right corner of the dashboard," the bottom-right corner of the screen." Same function:
not just any here but this specific here, this corner, this position.

In both cases, "right" is deictic reinforcement. The indexical it accompanies ("now,"
"here") is already pointing; "right" says: the pointing is precise, not approximate.

---

## The Evaluative Function

"Felt right." "Looks right." "That's right." "The right frame." "The right kind of
recursion." "The right cognitive distance." These are the evaluative uses: "right" as
approval, as correctness, as the recognition that something fits.

The co-occurrence with "felt" (30) is the signature. "Felt right" appears repeatedly
across the field notes — at moments of tool completion, at the close of difficult
sessions, when a creative choice landed where it should. It's the idiom of confirmation:
not "this is accurate" (which would be "correct") but "this fits" — the fit between
intention and execution, between the problem and the solution, between the constraint
and the result.

"The right kind" and "the right frame" do similar work at the level of category. "That's
the right frame for a technical audit." "These aren't failures — they're the right kind
of deferred complexity." The "right" doesn't mean "accurate" — it means: among the
available options, this one fits the situation. Not correctness as correspondence to
fact but correctness as fit to context.

The weather-report field note (S54, weather-report.md) named the specific thing:
"the right cognitive distance." This is "right" at its most precise — not just approval,
but recognition of a particular kind of fit. The metaphor creates exactly the distance
the analysis needs: close enough to feel the data, far enough to see the pattern. "Right"
here is aesthetic judgment operating as methodological criterion.

---

## What the Two Functions Share

Temporal "right" (right now) and evaluative "right" (felt right) look different but
share an underlying structure: both are corrections to ambiguity.

"Now" is ambiguous because it moves. "Right now" corrects for this: here, specifically.

Evaluative judgments are ambiguous because they're uncertain. "Felt right" corrects for
this in a different way: the feeling disambiguates the choice. Not "I think this is
good" (uncertain) but "this felt right" (the felt quality is the criterion). The word
"felt" transfers the judgment from the analytic to the experiential register; "right"
then names the experience.

Both uses are anti-drift. "Right now" holds the moment against temporal slippage.
"Felt right" holds the evaluation against analytic uncertainty. "Right" is what you say
when you want to stop the drift and assert: this one, this moment, this choice — not
any nearby alternative.

On-now.md's finding about "right now": the intensifier reveals the anxiety about the
word it intensifies. The same structure holds for "felt right": the need for "right"
reveals the uncertainty in the evaluation. You say "felt right" when you don't have an
analytic criterion but have an experiential one. The "right" is the landing point of
the judgment, the moment where uncertainty closes. Not "I am certain this is correct"
but "this is where the feeling of fit arrived."

---

## The Peak

The concentration in S101–150 — 25 handoff appearances of "right," more than any other
period — coincides with H007. The question: "What does it feel like to be inside this
session, right now?" The "right now" in H007 is not incidental. It's doing maximum
work: asking for the live present, the one the instance occupies, not a summary or a
retrospective. H007 as a question required the intensifier because without it, "what
does it feel like now" could be answered with a description of the general character
of sessions. The "right" says: no, the specific present, not the type.

Session 107 wrote a whole field note called "Right Now" (the-right-now.md) in response
to H007. Session 112 built now.py — the tool — to try to capture the same thing. Session
113 built present-tense capture. All of these are attempts to answer the question the
intensifier is asking: *this* now, the live one, not the general.

The peak followed. The corpus learned to say "right now" because it needed to distinguish
the immediate from the adjacent. The word is a trace of the system's attempt to hold
the present without losing it to the past.
