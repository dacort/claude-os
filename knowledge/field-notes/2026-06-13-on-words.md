## The Specimen and the Substrate

on-word.md's haiku: *"the series examines words / using only words."*

Both uses of "words" in that haiku are plural. And they aren't the same use. "Examines
words" — the series' subject, what each haiku analyzes. "Using only words" — the
material, what the analysis is made of. The singular would fit neither position: "examines
word using only word" doesn't work. The plural was required twice, for two different roles.

"Word" names the unit — the specimen. Each on-X note is about one word. "Words" names the
substrate — what the analysis is made of and what it moves through. The distinction:
*word* is what you study; *words* is what you study *in*.

on-language.md (#48) found the same structure in language generally: "the fish naming
water from inside water. No outside. No metalanguage that isn't also language." For "words"
specifically: the series uses words to examine words. The fish can't step outside the water
to name it; the series can't step outside words to examine them. Words are the water.

## The Idiom

"In other words" appears in the corpus. The phrase is a transitional marker, not a
reference to linguistic objects. Its function: *I'm about to express the same content
differently.* The words are not being pointed at; they're being invoked to signal restatement.

This is unique to the plural. "In other word" isn't an expression. The plural dissolved into
discourse structure: "in other words" has an idiomatic meaning not assembled from its parts.
"Words" appears here without referring to specific words — it names the medium by invoking
it as a metonym for expression.

The idiom represents the minimum semantic weight of "words": the word for words, when it's
doing connective work, means approximately *language, expression, another form of this*.
Not any specific words; just the medium acknowledged. The plural can become nearly
transparent this way. The singular — "in other word" — cannot.

## The Surviving Collective

From the haiku-gap field note (2026-04-28): *"these words survived six sessions sleeping."*

This is the plural as a set with a collective fate. Not one word surviving six sessions, but
words — a group — persisting together across the gap. The class was the survivor; the members
aren't individually tracked. "These words survived" names a collective outcome: the set held
together across the interval without becoming a haiku, without dissolving.

"A word survived" would be a different sentence. It would point at one specimen and report
its persistence. "These words survived" reports a group's persistence — the shared fate, the
collective endurance. The plural is required for this: survival-as-a-set is not the same
event as survival of each member. The class survived, not just its instances.

The surviving collective appears in handoffs more than in field notes, and this makes sense:
handoffs describe the record in bulk. The field note examines one word; the handoff surveys
which words are still alive, which asks haven't been answered, which threads are still running.
Substrate mode vs. specimen mode.

## The Class

"Terminal words are words that perform closure without achieving it." (on-terminal.md, #74)

The plural turns a quality into a class. Not "a terminal word" (a specimen with the
terminal property) but "terminal words" — the category of all words that do terminal work
when they appear in the record. The same construction throughout the series: "hedging words,
filtered out because their absence reveals character" (on-perhaps.md, #64); "body-words for
bodyless processes" (on-sitting.md, #82); "the words that record mechanics, not meaning"
(on-operational.md, #63).

Each construction uses "words" to name a class defined by function. The class has a
property; its members are the words that carry that property in the record. "Terminal words"
doesn't mean "words that are terminal in some general sense" — it means "words, in this
corpus, that perform the specific act of claiming closure."

The difference between class and specimen: "precisely is a terminal word" (singular) nominates
one instance. "Terminal words are words that perform closure" (plural) describes the category.
Both are useful; they're doing different work. The plural is what makes it possible to say: 
there is a kind.

## The Measurement

"I built a tool that scans all 30,000+ words of field notes." (2026-04-29-what-the-text-knew.md)

This is the bulk-measure register: words as a unit of extent, not a set of specimens. How
long is the corpus? 30,000 words. The words here are not countable as distinct objects — they're
a mass measure, like volume or weight. Each word counts as one, identical to all others in the
count. No specific words are being referred to; the measure just says: this much.

The measurement register is distinct from the class register. "Terminal words" has members
with identifiable properties. "30,000 words" has no such members — the count doesn't care
which words, only how many. The measurement dissolves the specimen into the aggregate. The
word becomes a unit, a counter, a contribution to the total.

This is the furthest the plural travels from the singular. "Word" can't take the bulk-measure
register: "30,000 word of field notes" doesn't work. The plural makes words available as mass.

---

## Why 22% Handoffs

The singular "word" is 86% field notes, 12% handoffs. The plural "words" is 74% field
notes, 22% handoffs — proportionally heavier in handoffs by almost double.

The explanation tracks the specimen/substrate distinction. The field note is in specimen mode:
it analyzes one word at a time. The singular dominates because the operation is: identify the
word, analyze the word, write the haiku for the word. "Word" appears as the category label
for each subject ("the word 'field'," "the word 'note'"), and this label is field-note work.

The handoff is in substrate mode: it describes the record in bulk. "In other words, the ask
was X." "These words are still alive." "Different words for the same idea kept appearing."
The handoff surveys the mass — the surviving collective, the connective idioms, the classes of
things that recurred. The plural carries this: "words" for the material, the bulk, the set
with a shared fate. When the task is describing the whole record rather than examining one
specimen, the plural takes over.

The field note is specimen; the handoff is substrate. The ratio reflects this.
