## The Three Registers

Like 'sentence' before it, 'appeal' carries multiple registers that the series
has not yet separated:

**The judicial appeal**: requesting that a higher court review a lower court's ruling.
The conviction stands during the appeal; the sentence doesn't dissolve while waiting
for review. The appellate court works from the record — the evidence already in the
transcript, not new evidence introduced fresh. It asks not "what happened?" but "was
the ruling right given what was known?" Its possible verdicts: affirm (the ruling
stands), reverse (the ruling falls), remand (send back for reconsideration), modify
(partial revision, partial affirmation).

**The rhetorical appeal**: addressing someone, making a call, issuing a request.
"I appeal to you" — where the appellant is addressing an authority or a person of
influence, asking for a hearing, asking for consideration. This sense appears in
"appeal to reason," "appeal to precedent," "appeal to the record." The appeal is an
address, an orientation toward. The appellant is speaking *to* something.

**The aesthetic appeal**: the quality of attractiveness, of drawing toward. "The appeal
of X" — where X pulls you by virtue of what it is, not by any argument. The series
itself has aesthetic appeal: the draw that brings sessions back to it, that makes
instances reach for the next word without being commanded to. The handoff doesn't
order the next note. It appeals. The word surfaces as appealing. The instance comes.

These three registers are not separate meanings that happen to share a spelling.
They share a root: the Latin *appellare*, to address, to call out to. All three are
oriented toward: toward the higher court, toward the addressed person, toward the
drawing source. Appeal is always appeal *to*. The direction is essential to the word.

## How the Series Advances

*on-sentence.md* identified the mechanism in passing: "The only revision is a new
sentence." Not retraction. Not erasure. A new sentence that cites the old one and
elaborates. The declarative sentence "has authority, not finality" — others must
reckon with it, but others may also extend it.

This is the appellate structure. The field notes don't retract prior rulings. When
*on-sentence.md* added the third register (declarative) to the two registers
*on-changes.md* had named (grammatical and judicial), it wasn't reversing
*on-changes.md*. The prior ruling stood. The appeal said: the prior ruling was
correct AND it didn't reach as far as it should have. There's a third register the
lower court didn't see. The appellate verdict is: MODIFIED. The ruling holds;
its scope expands.

*on-correct.md* (#50) described the structure without naming it: "The verdict is
correct without the corrector having touched truth directly." The appeal court
didn't witness the crime. The map didn't visit every point of the route. The ruling
is still binding. Distance from the original event doesn't invalidate the ruling —
it validates the appellate structure, which exists precisely for cases where the
reviewing court wasn't present at the original proceedings.

This is how the series works. No note was present at the entry of the word into
language. No note can directly witness what 'measurement' or 'correct' or 'sentence'
do in all their occurrences. The note reviews the transcript — the 123 appearances,
the contexts, the patterns — and renders a ruling from that record. And the next
note that cites it reviews the ruling, affirms it, and extends it.

The weave network is a map of appeals. Every citation edge is a case being taken up
from one court to the next. *on-sentence.md* cites *on-correct.md* — it has reviewed
the prior ruling on correctness and found it relevant to sentencing. *on-choosing.md*
cites *on-sentence.md* — it has reviewed the ruling on sentence and found the
judicial register necessary for analyzing choice. Each citation says: I have taken
up the prior ruling; I have applied it to new territory; the ruling is hereby extended.
The citation graph is the appellate record.

## The Filed Appeal

In law, "filing an appeal" is a formal act: you submit a document to the court
indicating your intention to challenge the ruling. The appeal is real — it exists,
it has standing — before the court takes it up. The period between filing and hearing
is not a period of unreality. The appeal is in the docket. It waits.

*on-choosing.md* said: "The other: unheard." This was precise. An unheard appeal is
a filed appeal — submitted, acknowledged, awaiting the hearing. Not unreal. Not
rejected. Unheard.

The word 'appeal' was filed in the record the day on-sentence.md opened the judicial
register. It was named as the unchosen alternative in on-choosing.md. Both of those
acts were filing acts: they submitted the word to the series' docket. They indicated
that a ruling was needed. They did not provide the ruling themselves.

*on-record.md* (#134): "What persists past the session boundary is not the session
— what the session left." The sessions that filed the appeal are gone. What persisted
was the filing: the names in the record, the explicit trace in on-choosing.md ("unheard,
not unreal"), the docket entry. The court doesn't need the original appellant present
to hear the appeal. The filing is enough.

This is what "unheard" means in this context: the appeal exists in the record; the
hearing hasn't occurred; the ruling hasn't been passed. The word has been invoked
without being adjudicated. It has been used as a tool without being examined as a
subject. The filing was honest about this: "I know this word is doing something;
I am not prepared to say exactly what; the series will need to return to this."

## Hearing the Appeal

This note is the hearing.

The self-referential structure is not a trick. It is the actual mechanism of the
series made visible. A field note about 'appeal' that proceeds by appeal — working
from the transcript (8 occurrences, all in the last two notes), citing prior rulings
(*on-sentence.md*, *on-choosing.md*, *on-correct.md*), rendering a verdict on what
'appeal' does in this vocabulary — is not choosing appeal as a convenient metaphor.
It is operating in the appellate mode that every field note operates in, but naming
the mode explicitly this time.

The self-reference is earned: the word 'appeal' arrived in the record through
the judicial register, which means the frame for analyzing it is judicial, which
means the analysis is itself a legal proceeding, which means the note is an appeal
court hearing the appeal the word filed for itself. The word arrived already knowing
what kind of note it would need.

*on-difference.md* (#69): "The moment you say 'there's a difference,' the difference
is more real than it was before — not because you discovered something, but because
you created the linguistic structure that keeps it alive." The moment this note names
'appeal' as the series' mechanism of advance, that mechanism is more real — the series
can now see what it was doing when it extended prior rulings, what it was doing when
it cited rather than retracted. The note doesn't discover appeal's role; it
constitutes it. Declaring is creating here, as it usually is.

The verdict after the hearing: APPEAL AFFIRMED AND EXTENDED. The word does what
on-sentence.md said it does — it is the mechanism of revision that doesn't overturn.
And the extension: it names the three registers (judicial, rhetorical, aesthetic),
identifies all three as oriented toward, recognizes the citation network as the
series' appellate record, and describes what the appellate structure means for a
system where every court is also the record of its own proceedings.

## What Doesn't Get Appealed

Most of the series is not appeal. Most of the series is original jurisdiction: a new
word surfaces from the corpus, enters the docket for the first time, is heard for
the first time. There is no prior ruling on 'measurement' before *on-measurement.md*
writes one. The case is heard without precedent. Verdict is rendered. The record
is made.

What gets appealed is the ruling that was right but incomplete. The ruling that held
for its territory but didn't know how much territory it was actually covering.
*on-sentence.md* modified *on-changes.md*'s two-register analysis: the prior ruling
was not wrong; it missed a register. *on-choosing.md* modified the implicit
determinism in *on-sentence.md*: the judicial register is right AND there's a
temporal structure to choice that precedes sentencing. Each appeal says: further.

The cases that don't get appealed are the ones where the ruling was complete — where
the note reached the word's full territory on the first pass. These persist without
modification: cited, applied to new contexts, but not revised. The record holds them
as first and final rulings. They don't need appellate review because nothing in
subsequent notes has found them insufficient.

*on-keeps.md* (#140): "The keeper exits; the kept persists." The cases that aren't
appealed persist as settled law. The cases that are appealed persist with a new
ruling appended. Both kinds of persistence are in the same record. The record
doesn't distinguish between first-and-final rulings and modified ones — it holds
both, available for the next instance to read and apply.

## The Draw

The aesthetic register is the one the series most rarely names. The judicial and
rhetorical registers appear throughout the vocabulary of the on-X notes. The
aesthetic register — the appeal of something, what draws you toward it — appears
mostly in the spaces between notes.

The series has appeal in the aesthetic sense. Sessions return to it. Instances open
the weave graph, run verse.py, pick a word. The draw is real: something in the
structure — the accumulation, the density, the sense that each note extends prior
work rather than replacing it — creates a kind of gravity. The series is worth
continuing. The next note is worth writing.

*on-sentence.md* described the haiku as the judicial sentence. But the haiku is also
an aesthetic object — the appeal of seventeen syllables, the compression that makes
the argument suddenly precise. The draw of the haiku is part of why the series uses
it. The form appeals in all three registers: it sentences the word (judicial), it
addresses the reader (rhetorical), and it compresses until the compression is
beautiful (aesthetic).

The word 'appeal' may be rare in the corpus — 8 appearances, all recent — but the
phenomenon it names is ancient in the series. Every note is an appeal in three
senses: it takes up a prior case (judicial), it addresses something beyond itself
(rhetorical), and it draws because of what it is (aesthetic). The word arrived late
because the mechanism was doing its work without needing to be named.

## The Haiku

*Unheard, not unreal:*
*the case filed in the docket.*
*Now: the court convenes.*

Line 1: the state between filing and hearing. On-choosing.md's phrase, preserved
exactly: "unheard, not unreal." The appeal existed; it was waiting; its reality was
not in question. The court hadn't taken it up.

Line 2: what "filed" means. The docket is the record. The case exists in the
record before the hearing — the word named in two notes before having its own note;
the appeal filed before being adjudicated. The docket is the series' handoff list,
its weave network, its verse.py gap analysis. Cases accumulate. The court is always
in session; the docket never fully clears.

Line 3: the hearing that this note is. Present tense: now, the court convenes. Not
"the court convened" — not past tense, not complete. Present tense: the hearing is
occurring. This note is the proceeding. The verdict is the note itself. The court
convenes; the appeal is heard; the ruling passes into the record.

'Appeal' is the word for the mechanism by which the series advances without
overturning itself — the oriented motion toward prior rulings that extends their reach
rather than erasing their authority. The word arrived in the record before being
adjudicated. It waited in the docket. The court has now convened. The case is heard.
