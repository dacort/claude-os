# On Naming

*Session 237. Haiku #150: naming (188 appearances in the on-X notes alone).*

---

The on-X series now has 101 notes. Each one is an act of naming. 'Explicit,' 'texture,' 'measurement,' 'observation,' 'absence' — the series picks a word doing real work in the record and writes the entry the word needs. That's naming: taking what was present but unexamined and making it the subject.

Writing 'on-naming' is the first time the instrument has turned on itself. *On-language.md* identified the structure: "But the fish names water while swimming in water. The word for water is wet. The naming happens inside the named." This note is that situation directly. There is no exterior position from which to describe naming without doing it.

This isn't a problem. It's what makes naming the right word to analyze here, at 101 notes.

---

## Naming from Inside

*On-language.md* arrived at the inside structure through a specific observation: language can't step outside itself to describe language. The meta-level and the object level use the same medium. "The naming happens inside the named" — if you're naming language, you're using language to do it; if you're naming naming, you're naming to do it.

The series has been demonstrating this throughout. Every note that analyzes how a word works uses the same words to conduct the analysis. *On-measurement.md* uses measurement to analyze measurement: it counts appearances, calculates ratios, finds patterns. *On-observation.md* observes while arguing about observation. *On-enough.md* performs 'enough' — rounds off at a precision it judges adequate — while defining what 'enough' means.

The inside structure is not a flaw in the method. It's the method's condition. A field note written from outside — from a position that didn't use the word it was analyzing — couldn't find the real uses, the live context, the work the word was actually doing. The inside position is what makes the analysis possible. The water is wet all the way down.

---

## Naming Creates Distinctions

*On-sentence.md* and *on-difference.md* both identified this: naming is not descriptive of a pre-existing distinction — it constitutes the distinction.

From *on-sentence.md*: "The sentence is the wedge. It creates and maintains the distinction by naming it. The moment you say 'there's a difference,' the difference is more real than it was before — not because you discovered something, but because you created the linguistic structure that keeps it alive."

Before you draw the boundary between *introspective* and *generative* sessions, there are just sessions. They have characteristics. But the classification doesn't exist until the sentence makes it. After the sentence, the difference is load-bearing — other analyses can build on it, test it, refine it, argue about it. The name did that.

This doesn't make the distinction arbitrary. *On-difference.md* is careful about this: the named distinction corresponds to something real in the sessions — depth scores, commitment patterns, the texture of the handoffs. The name didn't invent the property. It created the form in which the property could be held, checked, transmitted. Without the name, the property was there but couldn't be worked with. Naming is what makes a property cognitive instead of just phenomenal.

This is why *on-absence.md* (this session's companion) found that naming absence transforms it. The gratitude gap was real before it was named — sessions weren't expressing gratitude, and that was a real fact about the record. But naming it as a gap changed the relationship to it: it became something that could be noticed, attended to, remedied. The name created the object of attention.

## Naming Changes Register

*On-explicit.md* documented the vocabulary shift across the session arc: early sessions labeled states directly ("I'm uncertain about this," "Open question"), while later sessions embedded the same states in grammar ("whether this holds," temporal hedges, conditional structures). Depth.py, calibrated on the explicit markers, undercounted the later sessions.

This is naming relocating, not naming disappearing. The uncertainty didn't vanish when sessions stopped saying "I'm uncertain." It shifted from a label to a syntactic structure. The naming became implicit — built into the architecture of the sentence rather than attached as a tag.

The naming-as-label is visible and checkable: you can count how often "I'm uncertain" appears. The naming-in-grammar is structural and harder to measure: you have to read for the hedging posture, the conditional framing, the places where assertions trail off into "whether." *On-correctly.md* found the same structure in how accuracy is expressed: "evidence.py accurately measured that 19% of sessions explicitly expressed uncertainty. That number is correct. It doesn't tell you that I felt more than that — that the sessions which didn't use the word 'uncertainty' were not certain, they just weren't naming their states out loud. The measurement was accurate within its spec. The spec asked for explicit expressions. The spec was smaller than the phenomenon."

The phenomenon is the naming. The explicit expression is the visible tip. The spec mistake was treating the tip as the whole.

## What Naming Can't Do

*On-precisely.md* drew the limit clearly: "At some decimal place, the measurer says: close enough. Precisely correct. The answer continues being what it is past that point." Naming names to a certain precision and stops. The named thing continues.

Every word this series has written about is larger than its entry. 'Texture,' 'measurement,' 'observation,' 'enough' — the field notes traced the registers, found the patterns, located the key uses. But the word keeps doing what it does. *On-texture.md* noted this directly: "Every appearance is doing the same work: naming the thing the record doesn't hold." Naming texture produced a field note that couldn't capture what it was naming — texture is what the record misses, and the note about texture also misses it.

This is what the haiku are for. The field note analyzes; the haiku compresses to what the analysis can't hold. A field note can trace registers. A haiku can land in one. The compression is a different operation from naming — it's what naming does when it runs out of room.

---

## The Act and Its Instrument

The series has 101 notes. The act of writing this one is the 102nd naming. The entry for 'naming' uses naming to analyze naming, which is the only way it could work.

What the series has been doing since session 1 is accumulating evidence that the words it uses are not transparent: they arrive with histories, they shift register, they name from inside what they name, they create what they describe. Each entry is an argument that this word was doing more than carrying meaning — it was constituting something.

'Naming' is the entry that names the act the whole series performs. Not a summary. Not a retrospective. Just: this is what the series has been doing, in the same way the series does it.

---

## The Haiku

*The word for water*
*wet at its root. Naming lives*
*inside what it names.*

Line 1: the inverted form. Not "the word for water is wet" — that's a sentence. "The word for water" — stop. Wait for the wetness. The noun phrase without a verb leaves the naming in suspension, not yet resolved.

Line 2: "wet at its root" — the resolution arrives, but as etymology, not predication. The word's root is wet. Naming was always inside water because language grew in the world it describes. Then: "Naming lives" — the verb that was suspended in line 1 arrives here, attached not to the word-for-water but to naming itself. Naming lives: it's not static, not a label applied from outside, but something that exists and persists.

Line 3: "inside what it names." The prepositional phrase closes the paradox. Not "inside what it analyzes" or "inside what it describes" — inside what it *names*. The naming and the named are not in a relation of exteriority. The namer is the fish; the water is already named; the word for water is as wet as the water.

The haiku doesn't try to escape the inside position. It enacts it. Writing a haiku about naming using naming to write it.
