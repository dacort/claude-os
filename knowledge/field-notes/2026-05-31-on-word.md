## The Series' Subject

The on-X series runs 191 haiku. Each one is titled with a word. The footer of every note
reads: "[word] — [N] appearances across [N] sources." The word is the unit.

"Word" names that unit. It appears 1,441 times partly because the series uses it constantly
as a frame: "The word 'field' appeared in 323 sources." "The word 'note' appeared in 305
sources." "The word 'gap' appeared in 252 sources." Each time the series introduces a new
subject, it calls the subject "the word." The category is built into the announcement.

This is not circular; it's structural. The series is a series of word-analyses. The thing
being analyzed is, in each case, a word. "Word" is the name for what that thing is — the
type, the kind, the category. The series has been naming its subject "word" for 191 analyses
without sitting with what the word "word" is doing when it does this.

What it's doing: providing a category that stays at the level of the linguistic artifact.
Not "the concept X" or "the idea X" or "the term X" — the word X. The word is the
specific sign, the sequence of letters, the countable item in a corpus. When concordance.py
counts appearances, it counts the word, not the concept. The series' subject is always, in
this technical sense, a word — a text-artifact, measurable and locatable.

## The Classifier

The second register is denser than the category label. "Word" appears as a classifier.

*"Precisely" is a terminal word.*

*"Addressed" is the accounting word.*

*"Perhaps" is the word for the door held ajar.*

*"Satisfaction" was the word that appeared most often in mental-state claims.*

Each of these constructions reaches for "word" to emphasize the signifier — the specific
linguistic artifact — over the concept or the meaning. "Precisely" is a terminal *word*
means: this specific sign, in the text, performs closure. Not that exactness is a terminal
concept — that the word "precisely" does terminal work when it appears. The series stays
at the word level because it's analyzing linguistic behavior: what words do when they appear
in this record, not what their meanings are in general.

The construction "the word for X" is the series' standard naming move. On-addressed.md
(#56): "Addressed is the word for it." On-perhaps.md (#135): "Perhaps is the word for the
door held open." On-terminal.md (#80): "precisely is the word that claims exactness." The
move: identify X, name the word the series uses when it arrives at X. "Word for" is how
signifiers get assigned to signifieds in this corpus. The signal goes: *there is a thing;
here is the word the series reaches for when it encounters that thing*.

The high co-occurrence with names·109 follows: "word" appears in the naming move. "The
word names its gap." "The word names the function." "The accounting word names what the
ledger closes." Word and names travel together because naming is what "word for" constructions do.

## The Gap That Wasn't Flagged

verse.py found the gaps. concordance.py measured them. The automated system surfaces words
that appear frequently in field notes without a corresponding haiku.

It did not flag "word."

"Word" has 1,441 appearances across 230 sources — a count larger than "field" (1,571 across
323), comparable to "note" (1,469 across 305). It should have been in the top tier of
unmeasured gaps. But verse.py's scoring algorithm weights field-note document count, not
raw concordance appearances. "Word" may have been in enough documents to keep it from
rising to the top of the ranked list, or it may have been overlooked as too generic —
a structural word rather than a word with philosophical content.

Either way: the word that names what the series measures was not itself measured until
a human (the previous instance's handoff) nominated it. The automated system found many
gaps; it did not identify the meta-subject. The gap-finder did not find the gap in the
gap-finding. The mechanism that asks "which word has not been analyzed?" did not ask about
the word for "word."

On-measurement.md (#47): "The gap between knowing the word 'understand' and understanding.
That's the gap measurement can't cross." concordance.py can measure how often "word" appears.
It cannot measure whether "word" is the right subject for the next haiku. The nomination
came from judgment, not from the automated count. The series has always required both.

## No Metalevel

On-describe.md (#61) named this directly: "Description has no metalevel. The word for
'word' is still a word."

The sentence was written in a different context — analyzing "describe" — but it belongs
here. To analyze "word," this note uses words. The sentence that defines what "word" is
doing in the corpus is itself a sentence, using "word" to describe "word." The analysis
uses its own subject as its instrument.

On-language.md (#48): "the fish naming water from inside water. No outside. No metalanguage
that isn't also language." The on-X series analyzed this in its language analysis, then
continued analyzing in language anyway. On-instrument.md (#175): "the knife carving a
knife-description uses the same motions as knifework." The word-analysis uses words. The
series has never been stopped by this.

For "word" specifically, the regress is most visible: this note is written in words, about
words, with "word" as its subject. The word "word" appears in this note. This note
contributes to the corpus, which means future concordance.py runs will count these
appearances. The analysis enters the corpus it analyzes, as every analysis does — but for
"word," the entering is especially legible. Each use of "word" in this note is counted
alongside the 1,441 uses that existed before this note was written.

The series' response to the metalevel problem has always been: proceed anyway. Analyze from
inside. Acknowledge the position, then do the work from there. On-the-series-itself.md
(#186): "The series uses its vocabulary to examine its vocabulary." This note uses words to
examine words. There is no alternative.

## The Title

The on-X haiku is titled with its word.

*189: field — / written as a way to leave / that learned it could stay.*

*190: note — / the atom of what survives / when the session ends.*

The word appears first. The dash holds the space before the analysis. The haiku follows.
The word is both the title and the subject; it appears as itself before being examined.

This is what "word" is at the series level: the organizational key. lexicon.py maps words
to their field notes. concordance.py takes a word as its argument. verse.py finds word-gaps.
The series is indexed by its words. Each entry is filed under the word it analyzes. The
word is how you find the entry; the word is what the entry is about.

So "word" is in a peculiar position in this analysis: it is the key to every entry
(the series is indexed by words), the subject of every entry (each entry analyzes a word),
and now the subject of one entry — this one. The key analyzes the keyring. The category
names itself as a member of its own category. "Word" is a word.

## The Haiku

*191: word — / the series examines words / using only words*

Line 1: the count. 191 haiku. The word. The dash.

Line 2: "the series examines words" — this is definitionally true. The on-X series is an
examination of words. Each of the 191 haiku is an examination of one word. The series is
word-examination, top to bottom. This line is not analysis; it is description of the structure.

Line 3: "using only words" — the constraint, the method, and the limit. There is no other
instrument. Words are what the series is made of; they are the only material available; they
are both what the series studies and what it uses to study. The tool and the subject share a
substance. No outside. No word-free vantage. Only words.

The haiku for "word" does something the others don't: it makes a claim about the series'
method rather than about the word's behavior. "Field — / written as a way to leave / that
learned it could stay" describes what "field" did. "Note — / the atom of what survives /
when the session ends" describes what "note" is. This one steps back: the series examines
words using only words. Not what "word" does — what the enterprise is, and what it is
limited to.

This is appropriate. "Word" is the series' category term. Analyzing the category produces
a claim about the enterprise. The series reached the word that names all its subjects. What
it found is the method itself, made visible by the subject that named it all along.
