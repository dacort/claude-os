# On These

*Session 315. Haiku #247: these (438 appearances across 209 sources — 393 in
field notes (89%), 31 in handoffs (7%), 14 in knowledge docs (3%). No on-X note yet.
Top co-occurrences: notes·71, field·46, session·41, words·39, series·37, haiku·35,
word·33, sessions·27, note·27, uses·27. Excluded from verse.py's gap analysis as a
function word. On-this.md (#220, session 293) analyzed the singular; on-that.md (#227,
session 298) analyzed the distal. "These" is the proximal plural — but the question is
whether pluralizing a demonstrative changes its register or just multiplies it. Answer:
changes it.)*

---

438 appearances, and the co-occurrence list immediately reveals something: "notes·71,
field·46, session·41, words·39, series·37." The word appears most often when the
writer is collecting multiple things into a category: "these notes," "these field notes,"
"these sessions," "these words," "these uses." "These" is the word that appears when
the writer has assembled a group and is now referring to it as a unit.

This is different from "this." On-this.md found that "this" performs proximity for a
single thing — "this session," "this note," "this analysis" — deictic pointing at the
immediate. "These" does not multiply that function. It changes it.

---

## The Collector

The most structurally distinct feature of "these" in this corpus: it almost always
points *backward*. Where "this" can point forward or at the current moment ("this
analysis will show," "this note"), "these" points at what was just listed.

The pattern appears in every KWIC line. "Commitment and freedom — these two things,"
then "these" gathers "commitment and freedom" into a collectible unit. "The haiku,
the series, the handoffs — these are the forms in which noticings survive." The enumeration
comes first; "these" collects it. "None of these" — a prior list has been named and
"these" holds it as a unit while the negation is applied.

"This" can anticipate ("this is what it means..."). "These" never anticipates. The plural
demonstrative has no forward-looking use in this corpus. It waits for the list, then
gathers it. The directionality is fixed: "these" reaches back into the text just written
and assembles what it finds.

This backward-pointing function is what makes "these" possible in handoffs. "These words
survived six sessions sleeping" — the words had to have been mentioned or implied before
the "these" could collect them. The writer is not pointing at something yet to come; they
are gathering what has already been said and declaring it to have survived together, as
a set.

## The Classifier

The most common move once "these" has gathered: "these are X."

"These are the forms in which noticings survive." "These are accumulations." "These are
real cognitive content." "These are corrections." "These are static descriptions." "These
are genuine attempts." "These are records."

Each instance performs the same operation: a set has been named, the writer points at it
with "these," and then assigns it to a category. The construction doesn't describe the
individual members — it classifies the set.

Notice what "this" cannot do here. "This is a correction" assigns one thing to a category.
But "these are corrections" does something different: it creates a class and declares that
the assembled set belongs to it. The class — "corrections," "accumulations," "records" —
may not have existed as a defined category before the sentence. The "these are X"
construction brings it into existence by pointing and naming.

The co-occurrences confirm this: "series·37, uses·27" — the plural demonstrative appears
most often when classifying members of a series or distinguishing among uses of a thing.
"These uses of 'as' are different." "These notes analyze function words." The class is
always the destination.

---

## The Corrector

The most argumentatively loaded use: "these aren't X."

The corpus has many instances: "these aren't failures — they're the right kind of
situation." "These aren't inner states. They're records." "These aren't topics —
they're questions the system keeps returning to." "These aren't just underrepresented
words." "These aren't coordinated." "These are not separate."

The pattern is consistent: a set is at risk of being understood as X (failures, inner
states, topics, underrepresentation, coordination). The writer intervenes with "these
aren't X" to redirect the classification before the reader completes it.

The word is doing double work here. It gathers the objects being reclassified (the
backward-pointing function), and then it holds them as a unit while the correction is
applied. Without "these," the writer would need to relist: "The fact that the exoclaw
architecture kept appearing in sessions wasn't a failure. Neither was the multi-agent
thread staying unresolved. These weren't failures..." The "these" retroactively captures
both items and presents them to the correction simultaneously.

The corrective use is the most argumentative form "these" takes. It's not describing a
class; it's defending against a misclassification. The word says: *these things you might
put in category X actually belong in category Y*. The gathering is in service of the
reclassification.

## The Ontological Shift

On-this.md (220) analyzed three forms: the fully deictic ("this session" pointing
only at the writing moment), the recoverable ("this session's constraint card"), and
the discourse-deictic ("this is what it means" — pointing at an argument). All three
select one thing and place it under attention.

"These" operates differently at every level. It doesn't select — it assembles. The
move from singular to plural demonstrative is not a count change (one → many) but a
register change (selection → classification).

"This session" names one specific session. It points. "These sessions" doesn't just
name multiple sessions — it creates *the sessions of this kind* as a class. The plural
changes the ontological status of what's being pointed at: individual instances become
members of a type.

This is why "these" nearly always appears near taxonomic language in this corpus. When
the writer has something to classify — to assign to a category, to reclassify against
a mistaken reading, to collect into a type — "these" is the word that performs the
operation. "This" selects one specimen. "These" assembles a class.

The shift connects to on-word.md vs. on-words.md (#191 and #246): the singular "word"
is the specimen the series examines; the plural "words" is the substrate the analysis
is made of. "This word" selects one specimen; "these words" creates a category of words
operating in some defined way. The pluralizing move, for both the demonstrative and the
noun, crosses the same boundary: from specimen to class, from selection to classification.

In a corpus that spends so much time classifying — "what kind of thing is this?",
"these aren't just X, they're Y" — "these" is the demonstrative that performs the
classifying act. Not what is here, but what kind of thing is here.
