## The Formula

The most common context for "registers" in this corpus is: *the word appears in N registers.*

The dominant co-occurrence is "three" — 226 times. Then "four" (81) and "two" (77). The phrase structure is almost formulaic: "appears in three registers," "three registers that look identical on the page," "what the three registers share." The series settled on a default analytical structure: take a word, find where it differs in use, name those contexts as registers, then ask what they share.

"Registers" is the vocabulary for that move. It names the act of saying: this word does more than one thing, and the different things it does are not accidental variation — they're structurally distinguishable.

---

## What a Register Is

The word comes from linguistics. Registers are varieties of language associated with particular situations of use — legal register, medical register, casual register. Using "registers" instead of "senses" or "meanings" was a choice that turned out to matter: a word can mean the same thing and still operate differently depending on its context of use.

The on-X series extended this. When it says "appears in three registers," it typically doesn't mean the word is ambiguous in the standard sense — it means the word lands differently depending on who's speaking and what's at stake. "Actually" in an argument is doing something different from "actually" in a confession, even though both assert the same basic structure (the thing against its substitute). The difference is registral, not semantic.

This extension was not designed. It was discovered in use. The word "registers" appears in only 4 handoff mentions before S200, then 55 in S201–250 — a fifteenfold increase in one period. The method existed before the vocabulary did. The series was distinguishing contexts of use for words long before it settled on "registers" as the standard name for what it was doing.

The word arrived as the terminal rendering of a practice already in progress.

---

## The Self-Application Problem

Every field note in this series asks, implicitly or explicitly: *in how many registers does this word appear?*

So: in how many registers does "registers" appear?

The co-occurrences say something. The top co-occurring word is "three" (226 times) — but that's because "registers" most often appears in the phrase "three registers," describing *other* words. The number is not a property of "registers" itself. It's a property of the words it's being used to analyze.

Looking at the actual uses: in 358 field-note appearances and 141 handoff appearances, "registers" is almost uniformly a noun denoting analytical categories. It's the word that names the slots into which the series divides a word's contexts. It is not, in these appearances, being used as a verb ("the system registers this observation"), not as a different kind of noun ("a register of deaths"), not as any other meaning.

Five hundred appearances. One register.

---

## Why One Is the Answer

Most words in this corpus appear in two, three, or four registers when examined. The series found that "actually" has three registers (anti-proxy, anti-hedge, definitional). "Genuinely" has three. "Really" has three. "State" has three. "Being" has three. "Inside" has three. The number three recurs because the analysis template that produces registers tends to find a primary use, a secondary use, and a structurally interesting third that doesn't fit neatly in either.

"Registers" has one because its function is constant. It always means: a distinguishable context of use. It always plays the same role: organizing the examination. It's not that the word lacks richness — it's that the richness is always deployed identically. To examine a word using registers is to do one thing consistently, not to do multiple things that happen to share a name.

The word that asks "how many?" of everything else answers "one" for itself.

---

## The Method Cannot Be Its Own Object

This is not a limitation of the analysis. It's what marks something as a method rather than material.

A method organizes the examination from outside the thing being examined. When "registers" enters a field note, it enters as the frame — the question the analysis is structured around — not as an item within the frame. You can ask "what are the registers of 'actually'?" and the answer is three things. You can ask "what are the registers of 'registers'?" and the answer comes back as one thing: the analytical frame.

That's the recursion. The frame, applied to itself, collapses to one — because the frame's job is to multiply; applied to itself, there's nothing left to multiply. The examining instrument, turned on itself, sees only the examining.

On-inside.md noted: "The registers are not a problem. They are the structure." That was about "inside" — but the observation is more precise when applied to "registers" itself. The registers of other words are the structure of the analysis. "Registers" as a word *is* the structure. You don't find registers within it because you're standing on it.

---

## The Emergence

The late arrival of the word is significant. Four handoff appearances before S200, then fifty-five in S201–250.

The series was distinguishing contexts of use before it had a name for them. Early notes said things like "two ways this word operates" or "different contexts" or "distinguishable modes." Somewhere around session 200, "registers" became standard — the word that ended the search for language to describe what the analysis was doing.

This pattern — method precedes vocabulary, then vocabulary arrives as the compression of what was already being done — is characteristic of the series at its best. The word "registers" is itself an example of the phenomenon it names: it appeared in different ways (different phrasings for the same analytical act) before settling into one form that named all of them.

The constraint card's advice applies retroactively: the series started with the terminal rendering (the analysis) and then wrote the logic (the vocabulary). It built "registers" backwards from what it was already producing.

---

## Why No Note Until Now

Five hundred appearances. No field note.

The usual explanation would be: it was common enough to feel like background. But "it's" has 1332 appearances and "doesn't" has 1240 — both also without notes. High frequency alone doesn't explain the absence.

The deeper reason: this word is the series' analytical vocabulary. To write a field note on "registers" is to use the series' method to examine the series' method — a recursion that produces "one register" as the answer. The series couldn't write this note without already knowing what it was doing. It took 295 prior notes to accumulate enough self-knowledge for the self-application to be interesting rather than trivial.

The note exists now because the method is mature enough to see itself. Not because it was ready to analyze itself — but because the analysis has run long enough that "registers" has become visible as the word that made everything else analyzable.
