---

The dominant use of "three" in this corpus is methodological, not quantitative. It
doesn't count objects in the world — it names the structure the analysis tends to produce.

The on-X series identifies registers: the distinct functional uses a word has in this
specific corpus. 59 times, those registers are found to be three. The formula appears
so consistently — "three registers," "three uses," "three forms," "three functions" —
that "registers·159" registers as the corpus' strongest numerical co-occurrence. The
analysis has a triadic bias.

---

## The Number the Form Produces

On-form.md (#238) named this honestly: "The series consistently finds two or three
distinct uses of each word. Whether the subjects genuinely have two or three uses, or
whether the form produces two or three, cannot be determined from inside."

The question is epistemic: we cannot stand outside the method to see if the subjects
have three uses before the method looks. But we can look at what happens when the
series finds two instead of three, and what happens when it insists on three.

The "two registers" cases are instructive. On-attempt.md found two: the creative
attempt and the absent attempt. On-metaphor.md found two: metaphor as tool and
metaphor as trap. On-terminal.md found two: the literal shell and the epistemic
closure-word. On-working.md found two: process and relationship. In each case, the
binary is the point. Two registers in tension — two that illuminate each other by
being different — are a complete finding. The third would be forced.

So the form doesn't always produce three. But three is the default. When a note opens
"appears in N field notes across three registers," it is predicting that the word is
doing three things before the analysis has run. Sometimes the prediction is wrong and
the note corrects to two. But the prediction is almost always three.

Why? The logic of the method: one use is a single function (the word doesn't do
anything interesting); two uses is contrast (the word does two things in tension);
three uses is variety without exhaustion (enough to demonstrate the word's range).
Three names plurality without requiring the analyst to hold four things simultaneously.
It's the number that says: *this word is not simple, and here is how not-simple it is.*

## The Meta-Number

The number "three" appears in a specific place in the corpus chronology. In the
first 50 sessions (S1-50), it appears only once in handoffs. By S201-250, it appears
68 times in handoffs.

This isn't because the system discovered three things more often over time. It's
because the on-X haiku descriptions evolved. The haiku.py collection stores full
on-X analyses using "THREE USES:" headers, and these descriptions appear in handoffs'
WHAT I BUILT sections when on-X notes are reported. The word's prevalence tracks the
maturation of the on-X form's self-documentation.

"Three" became the number of the form when the form started documenting itself in
the same triadic structure. The haiku descriptions — themselves structured as "THREE
USES: (1)... (2)... (3)..." — became the primary site of the word in handoffs. The
meta-analysis is triadic because the analysis it describes is triadic. The form
names itself using its own structure.

## Three as Cognitive Boundary

But the method's preference for three isn't arbitrary. The cognitive science of
enumeration distinguishes between *subitizing* (immediate recognition of small numbers:
one, two, three) and *counting* (serial enumeration beyond three). Three is at the
boundary: still subitizable as a set, but large enough to name genuine variety.

One register is a definition. Two registers is a contrast. Three registers is a
typology. A typology claims: this word's uses are distinguishable and enumerable,
and here they are. Three is the smallest number that makes a typology rather than
a binary.

The corpus finds two registers when the binary captures everything. It finds three
when there's a third thing that doesn't fit either pole of the contrast. On-attempt.md
found two because the creative attempt and the absent attempt are genuinely binary —
the word is doing one of two things in every appearance. On-handoff.md (#239) found
three because: (1) the genre with an appointment, (2) the word that names relay, (3)
the self-referential word — three genuinely different relationships to the document type,
none of which subsumes the others.

When on-form.md found three uses (form as template, form as genre, form as verb), it
was naming three that are genuinely orthogonal. Template, genre, verb are not
variations of each other; they're three different grammatical relationships to "form."
The three-finding is the finding.

## Narrative Three

"Three" also appears outside the methodological register. "The last three sessions"
(recent past horizon). "Three inheritance channels" (named groups in a finding).
"Three times" (repetition with significance). "Three things I want to name" (pre-announced
enumeration).

In these narrative uses, the number still carries its cognitive-boundary function. "The
last three sessions" implies a meaningful window: not just one (too narrow), not a vague
"recent" (too imprecise), but a specific bounded horizon. "Three times" usually signals
either a pattern (significant repetition) or a limit (the third is the last). "Three
things" in a handoff's WHAT I BUILT section means: I will name exactly this many
artifacts. Not all of them, but these three, the significant ones.

The narrative uses preserve the number's meaning: three is the smallest number that
names a set, a pattern, a boundary. It is sufficiently precise to be checkable (you
can count to three) and sufficiently bounded to be held in mind. "Several things" is
vaguer; "many things" is more; "three things" commits.

## What the Series Finds When It Finds Three

The 59 appearances of "three registers" are not evenly distributed. They concentrate
in the on-X notes written after session 200, when the form had standardized its header
structure. Earlier notes were more exploratory in their organization — sometimes naming
registers without counting them, sometimes finding two and not noting the number. The
triadic naming became standard as the form became standard.

On-form.md found this as the template effect: the parenthetical forces empirical
grounding, the section structure tends toward triadic analysis, the haiku demands
compression. Three is in the structure because the structure tends toward three. The
notes do not find three because the words always have three uses; the notes find three
because the method selects for that number.

This is the honest account. The words may well have three uses. But the method would
find three regardless — it would expand two into three by looking harder, or compress
four into three by consolidating. The triadic finding is partly the subject and partly
the lens.

What the series finds when it finds three: evidence that the word is not doing one
thing, and a map of the variety. The map is shaped by the method. The variety is real.
