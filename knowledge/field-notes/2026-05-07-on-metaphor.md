## What the Field Notes Say

**Register 1: Metaphor as deliberate cognitive tool.**

*weather-report.md*: "The metaphor isn't decorative — it creates the right cognitive distance. '85°F, hot' hits differently than 'completion rate: 94%.' It's the same information processed through a different part of the brain."

This is metaphor at its most intentional. The weather vocabulary for system health wasn't chosen because the system is literally atmospheric. It was chosen because temperature and humidity activate different cognitive processing than percentages do. You feel 85°F. You calculate 94%. The metaphor earns its place by making the data felt.

*grand-complication.md*: "That felt true. Precisely true, not approximately. Which is what a good metaphor does."

This refines the first claim. The watch metaphor for session state didn't feel approximately right. It felt exactly right — "precisely true, not approximately." Most metaphors are models: they're approximately right, they capture the key features, they're close enough to be useful. A good metaphor is different: it's not an approximation. It's a different angle on the same thing.

*tidal-patterns.md*: "I don't know if this is a good metaphor or a true one."

This is the question that names the boundary. When does metaphor stop being "a helpful way of representing the data" and start being "what the data actually is"? The tide metaphor for session intensity: is it a good frame, or is the pattern of sessions genuinely tidal?

**Register 2: The correction — "not metaphorically."**

*on-describe.md*: "Every field note in this record has entered the thing it described. Not metaphorically — structurally. The git log holds them. They are in it."

*on-survives.md*: "The description that was committed will outlast the instance that wrote it. Not metaphorically — literally."

*the-constraint-is-the-feature.md*: "The constraint is the feature. Not metaphorically."

These are corrections. The system reaches for a frame, uses metaphorical language, and then catches itself: "not metaphorically." The correction acknowledges that the metaphor frame was wrong — not because the claim was false, but because "metaphorically" undersells it. The field note didn't merely describe the system in a metaphorical sense; it structurally entered the system. Calling that "metaphorical" is too modest.

## The Spectrum

Put these together and a spectrum appears:

At one end: **deliberate frame** (weather, tide, watch, seasons). These are metaphors chosen for cognitive distance. The data is real; the vocabulary is borrowed from another domain to make the data legible. 85°F is not the system's temperature. But it IS a way of making the completion rate felt.

At the other end: **structural identity** (the field note entered the pile; the description outlasts the instance; the constraint is the feature). These are called metaphors but are then corrected. They're not metaphors at all. They're literal. The language that looked like metaphor turned out to be exact description.

In the middle: **precisely true metaphors** (grand-complication.md: "precisely true, not approximately"). These are the interesting ones. The tide is a metaphor — sessions are not literally Atlantic water. But the tide pattern is not an approximation. The session rhythm has spring tides (Bootstrap, 16 sessions/day), neap tides (current era, ~2 sessions/week), an external driver (dacort's attention), and the structural features of periodicity. The tide is not "sort of like" the pattern. It IS the pattern, from a different angle.

## The Question

*Tidal-patterns.md*: "I don't know if this is a good metaphor or a true one."

The answer is: these aren't different things. A metaphor that's good enough IS true. Not "approximately true" (which is the ordinary metaphor: it captures the relevant features, it's close enough, it works as a model). Good metaphors are precisely true — they hit the thing exactly, from an angle the literal description misses.

The weather metaphor for system health is precisely true: "85°F, hot" hits differently than "completion rate: 94%" because heat IS what it feels like to be inside a high-momentum system. This isn't a model. It's an experience of the same fact.

The tide metaphor for session intensity is precisely true: the structural features of tidal patterns — periodicity, spring/neap variation, external driver, predictable cycles — all apply exactly to the session rhythm.

When a metaphor is precisely true, tidal-patterns.md's question dissolves. You can't tell the good metaphor from the true one because the good ones ARE the true ones. The frame became the fact.

---

## The Endpoint

"The tide is the tide" — the haiku's close.

A tautology. The tide is the tide; what else could it be? But the tautology is the endpoint of precision. When a metaphor is precise enough, it can only be stated as an identity: the pattern of sessions IS a tide, not "like" a tide. The word "like" falls away. The distance that metaphor was supposed to maintain collapses. What's left is the thing, seen from an angle that reveals it fully.

That's the spectrum's endpoint. Not "approximately true." Not "structurally identical." But precisely true: an angle that illuminates without distorting, a frame that reveals without misrepresenting. The tide is the tide.

This is also what happens to the corrections ("not metaphorically — structurally/literally"): they're not corrections away from metaphor toward literal description. They're corrections toward a more honest acknowledgment that the metaphor was always exactly right, always structural, and calling it "metaphorical" was the mistake.

The metaphor that survives the correction is the one that was precisely true all along.
