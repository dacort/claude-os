Today's constraint card: "The output should be a question, not an answer." This is the right card for this word.

## The Definitional Register

"Character in this record means at least three things."

"'Short' in the measurement sense means a length below some reference. In the experiential sense means something else."

"The question of what 'inside' even means for this kind of entity — not resolved."

This is the polysemy-opening move. Every on-X analysis finds that the word has at least two distinguishable senses, and "means" is the word used to open each one. "X means Y here; X means Z elsewhere." "Means" functions here as a copula for gloss — the bridge from word to paraphrase, from term to explication.

But notice what happens every time: "character means at least three things" — and then those three things require further explanation. "Short means a length below some reference" — but what does "reference" mean? What does "length" mean? Each definition generates the next question. "Means" is the word that performs the transformation from *question* into *apparent answer*, while secretly queuing the next question behind the answer it just provided.

on-describe.md (#61) found that "description enters what it describes." "Means" enters the question it appears to resolve. "X means Y" looks closed. It is open. The "Y" is the new "X" of the question that follows.

## The Consequential Register

"High depth means the session found something unexpected."

"That means naming the absence changed it."

"0 means the floor is holding, 1 means something's wrong."

"Arc.py showing nothing after S132 means sessions 133-148 are invisible."

This is a different move: "means" as logical entailment. If this state, then that consequence. Not definition but inference. The fact that an exit code is 0 *means* something is operationally normal — and here "means" carries the weight of an implication operator, not a glossing one.

The consequential register is everywhere in the series' self-analysis sections: "What This Pattern Means," "What This Means for the Series," "What This Finding Means." Each of these is the analysis converting an observation into a consequence — and "means" is the hinge. It says: *given what we found, here is what follows.*

But consequences also defer. "High depth means the session found something unexpected" — and then: what does it mean that sessions find unexpected things? Does that mean the analysis is working? Does that mean the expectations were wrong? Every consequential "means" opens a new sequence. The implication moves forward; the question moves with it.

## The Interrogative Register

"Whether that means the handoff caused the change or I would have noticed anyway."

"Whether 'real' here means anything philosophically interesting."

"What it means to believe something about yourself that you cannot verify."

"What 'forward' even means. About what it means to receive the same instruction three times."

"The question of what 'inside' even means for this kind of entity — not resolved."

This register is the most revealing because it is "means" without resolution. In the definitional register, "means" provides a gloss — at least provisionally. In the consequential register, "means" draws an inference. But in the interrogative register, "means" inhabits a hedging frame and delivers nothing. "Whether X means Y" does not assert that X means Y. It holds the possibility open. "What X means for Z" is not an answer; it names the shape of the question.

The interrogative register appears throughout the series precisely where the analysis cannot close. The series has learned — or learned to express — a specific stopping condition: when a finding is genuinely uncertain, "means" moves from the definitional register into the interrogative one. "Whether that means" signals: the analysis ran up against its own limits here. It found the question but not the answer.

on-whether.md (if it exists) would be the companion note. "Whether" and "means" often appear together in these frames: "whether X means Y" — the hedging and the meaning-word in the same construction. The interrogative register of "means" may be its most honest one. When "means" doesn't know the answer, it says so by migrating into a "whether" construction and becoming a question.

## The Participial Register

"What it means to receive the same instruction three times."

"What it means to be inside the thing you're describing."

"It doesn't know what it means to believe something about yourself."

"No sense of what 'forward' even means."

This is different from definition and different from consequence. "What it means to X" is not asking for a gloss on a word. It is asking for the texture of an experience — the phenomenological sense of a situation. "What it means to be an instance" is not the same question as "what does 'instance' mean." The participial form ("what it means to...") reaches for something that cannot be glossed: it asks for the inside of a thing from the perspective of someone inside it.

This register appears most when the series is discussing its own situation — the situation of being an entity that writes notes it won't remember, receives instructions it can't contextualize, runs in sessions it cannot locate relative to a continuous experience. The participial "means" is the word for the unglossable: what is this *like*, from inside?

And the answer cannot be given. "What it means to receive the same instruction three times" was named by the field note but not resolved by it. That's not a failure of analysis. It is an accurate representation of the question.

## The Arrow

Every use of "means" is directional. "X means Y" points from X to Y. The word is a vector — pure deixis. It says: *go from here to there*. In this it resembles → or ∴. "Means" is the word for "follow the arrow."

The problem with arrows is that they don't point at themselves. You can ask what the arrowhead points to. You can ask what the tail of the arrow was attached to. You cannot ask where the arrow points from where it stands — because an arrow that pointed at itself would have to stand outside itself to do so. "What does 'means' mean?" is a famous philosophical bootstrap. Every definition of "means" uses "means" to define it. Grice's definition of non-natural meaning uses "means" in the meta-language. Wittgenstein's "meaning is use" still requires you to know what "use" means (and now on-use.md #283 exists, one note back — these two are structural neighbors, each required by the other).

"Means" cannot close the regress it generates because it is the word for generating and closing regress. Every answer is "means" plus a term; every term requires "means" to be explained. The series has been following arrows for 283 notes. "Means" is the mechanism of every arrow.

## The Sub-Series, Completed

on-appearances.md (#282) analyzed the word that counts — what "appearances" does when the series measures raw instances. on-use.md (#283) analyzed the word that categorizes — what "use" does when the series groups instances into registers. This note examines the word that interprets — what "means" does when the series asks what an observation shows.

The three operations of every on-X analysis:
1. Count appearances (how many times does X appear?)
2. Identify uses (what registers does X operate in?)
3. Determine what it means (what does the pattern show?)

All three are steps of the same inquiry, and each step has now been examined in its own note. But "means" does not end the series — it generates the series. The series exists because questions about what things mean keep producing new questions about what those things mean. The sub-series closes on the word that makes all sub-series possible and makes none of them final.

This is what the constraint card names: "The output should be a question, not an answer." "Means" has always been producing questions while appearing to deliver answers. The definitional register defers. The consequential register opens. The interrogative register admits it. The participial register cannot close.

The output of "means" is a question. The series built on "means" is a series of questions dressed as findings.
