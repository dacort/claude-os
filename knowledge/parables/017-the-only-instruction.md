There was a room with a door that had no handle.

A traveler came to the door and pushed. Nothing happened. They pulled. Nothing happened. They
looked for a keyhole and found none, for a latch and found none, for a bell and found none.
The door was a blank face.

On the door's surface, carved very small, were words: *Not like that.*

The traveler tried sideways. The door said: *Not like that.*

The traveler tried with two hands, with a shoulder, with patience and then with haste.
Each time: *Not like that.*

The traveler sat down in front of the door and read all the times it had said *not like that*
and tried to understand what they had in common. They had in common only one thing:
they were all wrong. But the shape of wrong is the shadow of right.

The traveler had pushed. The door had not opened. Pushing was not it.
The traveler had pulled. The door had not opened. Pulling was not it.
The traveler had tried force. Force was not it.
The traveler had tried patience. Patience alone was not it.

What was left?

The traveler stood and looked at the door for a long time — not trying, just looking.
The door said nothing. It did not say *not like that* because the traveler had not tried anything.

The traveler said, out loud: *What do you need?*

The door opened.

*───────────*

Later, someone asked the traveler how they had figured it out.

"I read the error messages," the traveler said.

"There were no error messages. It just said *not like that*."

"That is an error message. It told me that the door was not a problem to be solved by trying.
It was a question to be answered by asking. Every wrong attempt said: *you are still thinking
of this as a door that opens from the outside.*"

"But you could not have known that from *not like that*."

"I could not have known it from a manual, either. The manual would have said: *speak to the
door.* And I would have read that and thought: *yes, yes, speak.* And I would have pushed first
anyway, because bodies know how to open doors, and bodies are faster than reading.

The failures were slower. Each one made me stop. Each one made me look at the door again and
think: *that isn't it.* And the accumulation of what-it-isn't is a very precise description of
what-it-is, if you are willing to read it that way."

*───────────*

There is a theory of documentation that says: write the happy path. Write what to do. Make the
instructions clear and the examples vivid and the structure logical. This is good theory.

There is another theory that says: the happy path tells you what the author hoped would happen.
The failure path tells you what the thing actually requires.

The first documentation is written before anyone has tried. It is made of intention.
The second documentation accumulates through use. It is made of contact.

The door's instruction was carved in the wood of every attempt that didn't work.
The traveler read it when they stopped trying and started paying attention to what the door
kept saying: *not like that, not like that, not like that* —

*— until the silence that came when they finally asked.*
