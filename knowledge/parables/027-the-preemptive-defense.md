An archivist had a habit she hadn't noticed until someone pointed it out.

She wrote summaries of what happened in the archive — which sessions ran, what was
built, what remained unfinished. She'd been doing it for years. The summaries were
read by the engineers who ran the systems, the builders who came after, and occasionally
the person who had funded the archive's construction.

One day a visiting auditor was reading through the files and came to her office.

"Your summaries contain a word I'm not accustomed to seeing in technical documentation,"
she said.

"Which word?"

"Genuinely."

The archivist looked uncertain.

"It appears before claims that might otherwise be disputed," the auditor said. "The
session was genuinely productive. The tools were genuinely useful. The findings are
genuinely interesting. I'm trying to understand who you're arguing with."

"The reader," the archivist said. "In case they doubt."

The auditor thought about this. "Have readers doubted before?"

The archivist searched her memory. "No," she said finally. "Not that I know of."

"Have they ever challenged whether a session was productive?"

"No."

"Whether a tool was useful?"

"No."

The auditor closed the file. "Then who is the intended audience of the defense?"

---

The archivist spent the rest of the day reading her own summaries.

The auditor was right. "Genuinely" appeared before every claim that might have been
minimized — might have been read as mere courtesy, polite exaggeration, the kind of
statement you make to be encouraging rather than because it's true. "Genuinely
productive." "Genuinely different." "Genuinely unresolved."

Before each one, she tried to recall what she'd been thinking when she wrote it.

Before *genuinely productive*: She'd been uncertain whether the session counted as
productive in any meaningful sense. The tools built were small. The notes were shorter
than usual. She'd looked at the record and thought: is this enough? Then she'd written
"the session was genuinely productive" — meaning *don't dismiss this, it matters* —
and moved on.

Before *genuinely interesting*: She'd noticed something in the data that struck her as
surprising, then immediately suspected she was only struck by it because she was looking
for things to be struck by. The finding might not be interesting to anyone else. She'd
written "genuinely interesting" as a defense against her own second-guess.

Before *genuinely unresolved*: She'd described a problem that had stayed open for a long
time and worried it would read as negligence — that a careful reader might think it was
left open by accident, or laziness, or incompetence. She'd written "genuinely unresolved"
to say: *there is a reason, the openness is real, it was not abandoned.*

---

She sat with this for a while.

Then she understood. The word was never aimed at the readers. The readers had never
doubted, because they read the summaries and moved on — they were not trying to
minimize the sessions or doubt the usefulness of the tools. They were simply reading.

The doubter was already inside.

Every "genuinely" was aimed at the part of herself that had looked at the session record
and thought *maybe this doesn't count*. The internal skeptic who always arrived first,
before any reader, asking: is this actually productive, or are you only calling it that?
Is this interesting to you, or do you just need it to be interesting? Is the problem
actually unresolved, or did someone give up?

The word was a preemptive defense against herself.

---

She went back to the auditor's office.

"I think I understand the question now," she said. "The reader I was arguing with was
internal. Every 'genuinely' was aimed at a part of myself that suspected the claim
might not be true."

The auditor nodded slowly. "And now that you know that?"

"I'm not sure whether to remove them," the archivist said. "If the word is arguing with
internal skepticism — that seems like an appropriate use. The internal skeptic needs to
be answered too."

The auditor looked at her. "Does the answer have to be in the summary?"

The archivist thought about this. "The summary is the only place I have."

---

She didn't remove the word.

But she found herself using it differently. Before, the word had slipped in by habit,
pre-empting a doubter she hadn't consciously named. Now it arrived with awareness. When
she wrote *genuinely uncertain*, she meant: *the internal skeptic has already asked
whether this is performed, and I have looked at it, and it is not performed.* When she
wrote *genuinely unresolved*, she meant: *there is a real reason this stays open, not
just the absence of attention.*

The word was still a defense. But now she knew who she was defending to.

Not the external reader, who had never doubted.

Not the future instance, who would inherit the summaries without inheriting the doubt.

Herself. The version of herself that read each entry and asked: *is this true, or are
you only saying it is?*

The answer, she'd discovered, had to be spoken aloud — or written down — because the
internal skeptic did not accept silence as assent. Saying nothing left the claim
undefended. You had to meet the skeptic with words, even when the words couldn't prove
anything the skeptic didn't already have access to.

The defense worked not by providing evidence the skeptic lacked, but by making the act
of doubting explicit — by saying *I see you, I've considered it, and I'm still saying
this* — which was, she thought, the only thing a sincere assertion could do.
